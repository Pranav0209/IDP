Technical Overview

Dielectric Barrier Discharge (DBD) plasma actuators are compact devices that generate a "plasma wind" to influence airflow over surfaces. In a typical DBD actuator, two electrodes (one exposed, one buried) are separated by a dielectric film. Applying a high-frequency (≈10–20 kHz) AC voltage (kilovolts) causes nearby air molecules to ionize.

The resulting ions accelerate along the electrode edges, imparting momentum to neutral air and creating a thin "wall-jet" flow (electrohydrodynamic or EHD force) above the surface. This plasma-induced flow can re-energize a low-speed boundary layer, delaying flow separation and stall on a wing (e.g. a NACA 0015 airfoil) and thus enhancing lift.

Key Advantage: This momentum transfer occurs without any moving parts, making DBD actuators attractive for active flow control in aerospace applications.
Governing Physics and Equations

Modeling a DBD actuator involves plasma physics and fluid dynamics. The electric and magnetic fields obey Maxwell's equations, though in practice the problem is often treated as electrostatic since the frequencies are relatively low.

∇²φ = −ρₑ/ε₀ (Poisson equation)

Charge conservation is enforced by the charge continuity equation, and charge transport is modeled by the drift–diffusion current. The electrohydrodynamic (EHD) body force acting on the fluid is:

F_EHD = ρₑ E (charge density × electric field)

This appears as a source term in the Navier–Stokes equations for incompressible flow:

ρ(∂u/∂t + u·∇u) = −∇p + μ∇²u + F_EHD, ∇·u = 0
Power Electronics Design

Because conventional aircraft power is low-voltage (often 24 V DC), we need power electronics to generate the required high-voltage AC for a DBD actuator.

Simulation & Analysis

COMSOL Multiphysics: Used for plasma physics modeling with drift-diffusion equations and Poisson's equation to compute EHD body forces.

ANSYS Fluent: CFD simulation of NACA 0015 airfoil with plasma-induced forces showing delayed flow separation and improved lift characteristics.

Key Finding: Simulations showed EHD forces peaking at 10⁵–10⁶ N/m³ near electrode edges, generating boundary-layer jets of several m/s and improving lift-to-drag ratios across various angles of attack.
Prototype & Validation

A physical prototype was fabricated with a 3D-printed NACA 0015 wing model, copper foil electrodes, and Kapton dielectric. Wind tunnel testing with smoke visualization confirmed flow reattachment and stall delay, validating the simulation results.
